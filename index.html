The Flickering Planet

Stories of cities across the world as revealed from the trends in satellite's night data.

Seen from space, a city at night looks static but when we look at the same city night after night, month after month, year after year, and the picture starts to flicker. A sudden brightening might mean a new industrial zone has come online, or a wave of urban growth. A sudden dimming might mean a government cutting energy costs or a war knocking out power grids. Light, it turns out, is a surprisingly honest witness. It records economic booms, political crises, environmental policy, even the seasons. All in the same simple signal: how much light escapes into space each night.

This same data is also how scientists track light pollution, also called the Artificial Light at Night (ALAN). The glow spilling upward off our cities doesn't just look pretty from orbit; down on the ground, it washes out the night sky, disrupts wildlife, and interferes with human sleep patterns. So every map in this project is really telling two stories at once: a story about a city's stability, and a story about how much of the night sky that city is quietly erasing.

Eight places. Twelve years. Each light telling a different story — about economy, seasons, politics, and the sky above.

Black Marble comparing lights 2012 vs. 2016, source: https://svs.gsfc.nasa.gov/30886/

Every night, a NASA satellite measures that upward glow across the globe. Those nightly readings get pooled into one image per year — keeping only the clearest, moonlight-free nights, filtering out clouds and sensor noise. NASA calls the resulting dataset Black Marble. Every map on this page draws from it.

Each of the following maps shows radiance — how much light escapes upward from Earth's surface, measured in nanowatts per square centimeter per steradian (nW/cm²/sr). Brighter pixels mean more light; darker pixels mean less.

Permian Basin, Texas

Where the earth catches fire — first for oil, now for AI

2013

— nW/cm²/sr

Out in the plains of West Texas, satellite radiance surged as the shale oil boom took hold. A decade of horizontal drilling and fracking turned quiet ranchland into one of the fastest-growing oil regions on Earth. That climb wasn't uninterrupted: in 2020, oil prices collapsed as COVID-19 crushed global demand, and rigs across the Permian went idle almost overnight, a brief but sharp dip before drilling roared back. Much of that new light traces back to gas flaring, excess natural gas burned off at the wellhead instead of captured, an economic and environmental signal energy researchers have tracked for years.

More recently, that same cheap gas has found a second use: powering AI. Chevron and Microsoft are building a 2.67-gigawatt data center running entirely on Permian gas, and OpenAI's Stargate campus draws on the same supply network. On the map, Permian Basin doesn't just grow. It visibly catches fire, first for oil, now for AI.

India

A Decade of Light

2016 → 2025

In 2016, large parts of rural India were still dark after sunset. By 2025, much of that darkness had been switched on. Government electrification schemes launched from 2014 onward brought grid power to tens of thousands of villages, and a later push connected nearly 29 million rural households that had never had electricity before. The International Energy Agency has pointed to 2018 alone as a year in which India connected close to 100 million people, a pace on par with what it would take to close all of sub-Saharan Africa's electricity gap by 2030.

Independent researchers note that the government's later declaration of "100% household electrification" leaned on some generous redefinitions, and pockets of rural India still go without reliable power. But the two satellite images, nine years apart, tell the broad story clearly: hundreds of millions of people went from candlelight and kerosene lamps to a lit home in less than a decade.

Gujarat Coastal Corridor

The Gulf of Khambhat

2016 → 2025

Hugging India's western coastline, the Gulf of Khambhat traces the physical acceleration of industrial growth. Dahej, a deep-water port built specifically for chemicals and hazardous cargo, has become India's first dedicated chemical port and now anchors one of the country's largest Petroleum, Chemicals and Petrochemicals Investment Regions, hosting major players like Reliance, ONGC, GAIL, and Adani. Nearby Hazira handles LNG and container traffic feeding Gujarat's industrial hinterland. Together with expanding Special Economic Zones along the coast, this stretch of shoreline has been quietly building itself into one of India's most concentrated industrial engines.

Every map on this page starts here — with a small family of NASA-NOAA satellites that circle the Earth from pole to pole, passing over nearly the same spot every night. It began with Suomi NPP, launched in 2011, and continues today with JPSS-1 and JPSS-2. Together they carry an instrument called VIIRS, and specifically its Day/Night Band, a sensor sensitive enough to catch faint light sources like streetlights, even under moonlight.

One quirk worth knowing: this sensor sees light differently than our eyes do — more sensitive toward red and near-infrared than blue. It's why modern blue-skewing LEDs (see: Detroit) can look dimmer from space than they really are, and why snow-covered nights (see: Berlin) need their own correction before they're counted as real light.

This project was inspired by Satellite imagery reveals increasing volatility in human night-time activity (Li et al., Nature, 2026) — a paper that used the same Black Marble dataset to show that Earth's night lights aren't steadily brightening but constantly, simultaneously brightening and dimming across the globe. Kyiv, Paris, and the Permian Basin were among its own case studies.
